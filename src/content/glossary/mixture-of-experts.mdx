A <G term="transformer">transformer</G> variant where the feed-forward block is replaced by a set
of expert subnetworks plus a small router that picks the top-k experts
to run for each token. The model has high total parameter count but
activates only a slice of it per forward pass, so capacity and <G term="inference">inference</G>
FLOPs become independent dimensions. <G term="mixtral">Mixtral</G> 8x7B activates roughly 13B
of its 47B parameters per token; <G term="deepseek">DeepSeek</G>-V3 activates 37B of 671B.

Mechanically: every token's hidden state goes through a learned gating
function that outputs a softmax over experts; the top-k (usually 1 or
2) experts process the token, and their outputs are weighted-summed.
Training is harder than dense models because the router has to learn
which experts specialize in what, and idle experts get no gradient.
Auxiliary load-balancing losses prevent collapse into one or two
dominant experts.

In open-source AI the architecture matters because it offers a way to
push capacity without paying linear <G term="inference">inference</G> cost. <G term="mixtral">Mixtral</G>, <G term="deepseek">DeepSeek</G>,
Qwen3-MoE, and <G term="llama">Llama</G> 4's preview all use it. Runtimes serving MoE models
need expert parallelism (different GPUs hold different experts) which
makes them harder to serve at home but more efficient at fleet scale.

Distinct from `dense models` where every parameter activates every
forward pass, and from `model parallelism` which splits a dense model
across devices rather than routing through learned subnetworks.
