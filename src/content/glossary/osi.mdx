The standards body for what counts as "open source." Founded in 1998,
the OSI maintains the Open Source Definition (a ten-point checklist
descended from the Debian Free Software Guidelines) and approves
licenses that meet it. Vendors that want to claim a license is "OSI-
approved open source" submit it for review against the definition.

OSI's relevance for AI is the <G term="osaid">OSAID</G> work (<G term="osaid">Open Source AI Definition</G>,
released October 2024). It extends the principles of the Open Source
Definition to AI systems: weights are not enough; you also need
sufficient information about training data, code, and architecture so
a third party can study, modify, and reproduce the system.

The definition is contested. Major labs use "open source" as a
marketing term for weight releases that do not meet OSAID criteria;
the OSI has been clear that those releases are not OSAID-compliant
even if the term is widespread. Whether OSI's definition or the
broader market usage prevails is one of the live governance fights
through 2026.
