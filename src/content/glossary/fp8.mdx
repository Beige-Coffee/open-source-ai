An 8-bit floating-point number representation with two common variants:
E4M3 (4 exponent bits, 3 mantissa bits, more precision, less range)
and E5M2 (5 and 2, more range, less precision). FP8 emerged as a
practical <G term="inference">inference</G> precision on NVIDIA Hopper (the <G term="transformer">Transformer</G> Engine)
and is now common across Hopper, Blackwell, MI300X, and other 2024+
accelerators.

The motivation is straightforward. FP16 weights cost 2 bytes each;
FP8 costs 1 byte. A 70B-parameter model goes from 140 GB to 70 GB,
fitting comfortably on a single 80 GB H100. Memory bandwidth pressure
on <G term="attention">attention</G> and matmul halves at the same time, improving <G term="throughput">throughput</G>.

Training in FP8 is harder than <G term="inference">inference</G>: the lower precision makes
gradient updates noisier and stability touchier. <G term="deepseek">DeepSeek</G>-V3 reported
production FP8 training, and several open follow-ons have replicated
the technique. The default in 2026 is FP8 inference, mixed-precision
FP8/BF16 training, with full-precision rare.
