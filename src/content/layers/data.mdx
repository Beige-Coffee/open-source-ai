## What this layer is

The training corpora. The text, code, images, audio, and synthetic data
that go into pretraining and fine-tuning. This is the layer where "open"
is the most ambiguous, because almost every open-weights model has data
that is either secret (Llama, Mistral, Qwen, DeepSeek) or partially
disclosed (Falcon mentioned its sources; Llama 3 named some sources but
not the mix). Truly open data, meaning a published corpus you could
re-train on, exists for a small number of models. OLMo is the prototype:
AI2 released the weights, the training code, and the training corpus
(Dolma).

The data layer is where the OSAID v1.0 conversation lives and where the
Q4 2026 update is most likely to bite. Different definitions of "open"
disagree on whether the training data must be publicly available or
whether documentation of the data sufficient to reproduce the model's
data pipeline is enough.

## Open vs. closed

The open side is a relatively small set of public corpora. Common Crawl
is the substrate: petabytes of HTML and metadata crawled monthly since
2008. Every modern open corpus is a filtered subset of Common Crawl plus
some additional sources. The Pile (EleutherAI, 2020) was the first major
open dataset oriented toward LLM training. RedPajama (Together AI, 2023)
is an open replication of Llama 1's training mix. Dolma (AI2) is the
OLMo training corpus, around 3 trillion tokens. FineWeb (HuggingFace,
2024) is fifteen trillion tokens after filtering Common Crawl with
careful quality classifiers. DCLM is both a benchmark for data filtering
and the resulting filtered corpus.

The closed side is the data behind every frontier model, plus the
licensed data labs have paid for. OpenAI signed deals with Reddit, Stack
Exchange, Axel Springer, the Financial Times, Vox, Associated Press, and
others. Meta's training corpus included Library Genesis content, as
documented in legal filings. The data layer is where the legal and
economic structure of frontier AI gets least transparent.

A third category that matters for sovereignty: synthetic data. Models
are now used to generate the training data for the next generation of
models. Phi-3 was trained heavily on synthetic data. The implication is
that the closed-frontier-model output becomes the seed for the open
ecosystem, which is a soft form of upstream dependency that does not
show up in any open-source license.

## The lock-in vector

Data is a weaker lock-in vector than silicon or runtime, but it has two
real teeth. First, the legal one: licensed-data deals are non-transferable.
If OpenAI paid the Financial Times for training data, that data is not
available to an open project at any price. Second, the contamination one:
if open-weights models are trained on the output of closed models, the
open ecosystem is downstream of the closed ecosystem in a way that is
hard to escape.

The framing here: data is where the OSI / OSAID fight actually lives. You
cannot be "open source" without disclosed data. The push-back from labs
is that fully disclosed data is impossibly expensive given the licensing
landscape. The OLMo counter is that it is not impossible, just more
expensive in absolute terms, and labs prefer the cheaper path.
