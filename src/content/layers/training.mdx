## What this layer is

The tools that take open data plus an architecture and produce weights.
This is two related ecosystems. **Pretraining** infrastructure (Megatron-LM,
DeepSpeed, FSDP, AxLearn, JAX-based stacks) is what frontier labs and AI2
use to build base models from scratch. **Fine-tuning** infrastructure
(Unsloth, Axolotl, LLaMA-Factory, HuggingFace TRL) is what an individual
or a small team uses to take an existing open-weights base model and
adapt it to specific data, tasks, or domains.

Training is a layer in the production pipeline that the standard
nine-layer taxonomy historically smuggled into Weights or Runtime.
Giving it a dedicated layer makes the ecosystem visible: tools to make
training accessible to non-hyperscaler audiences are the single cleanest
sovereignty story in 2026. The cost of fine-tuning Llama 3 8B dropping
from "A100 only" to "RTX 4090 plus a consumer Mac" is what put model
customization within reach of any developer.

## Open vs. closed

The open side is most of what people actually use. **Unsloth** offers
2x-30x faster fine-tuning at 70% less VRAM than the HuggingFace TRL
baseline, with a free open-source tier and paid Pro / Enterprise tiers.
**Axolotl** is a popular general-purpose fine-tuning framework. **LLaMA-Factory**
is the Chinese-open-source equivalent, very feature-rich. **HuggingFace TRL**
is the general standard library for supervised fine-tuning and RLHF.
**Megatron-LM** (NVIDIA) and **DeepSpeed** (Microsoft) are the heavyweight
distributed-training frameworks, open source but historically tuned for
NVIDIA hardware. **FSDP** (PyTorch's Fully Sharded Data Parallel) is the
default for production-scale training in 2026.

A distinct sub-cluster within Training is **decentralized training**:
Prime Intellect's INTELLECT-1, Nous Research's DisTrO, Templar's
Covenant-72B, Gensyn, Pluralis. These projects use techniques like
asynchronous gradient sync and gradient compression to make
distributed-over-the-internet training feasible. They are still 10-100x
slower than centralized frontier training, but they are real and they
ship working models.

The closed side is what every frontier lab uses internally. We can guess
the shape (PyTorch + some custom stack + Megatron-style sharding) but
the specifics are proprietary.

## The lock-in vector

Training has minimal direct lock-in. The tools are open. The lock-in
shows up upstream (in Silicon, where NVIDIA's NCCL still dominates
distributed-training synchronization) and downstream (in Weights, where
the resulting model's license determines what you can do with the output).

The interesting question at this layer is reach: who can train, and what.
In 2026, the answer for fine-tuning is "anyone with a 4090 and a free
Colab notebook." The answer for pretraining frontier-class models is
still "labs with NVIDIA hyperscaler-class infrastructure." Decentralized
training is trying to flip the second answer; it is not there yet.
