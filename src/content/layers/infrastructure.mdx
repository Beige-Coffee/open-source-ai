Where the math has to physically happen. The buildings, the
electricity, the cooling, and the grid interconnect that determine
whether a multi-gigawatt training run can even start. This layer
sits below silicon (the chips themselves) and below compute (the
control-plane scheduling that runs on those chips); it is the
substrate the rest of the stack assumes. The question this layer
answers is whether the math gets done at all, and on whose land,
under whose regulator, drawing from whose grid.

## The constraint moved from chips to electricity

Through 2024, the binding constraint at the <G term="frontier">frontier</G> was <G term="gpu">GPU</G>
allocation. Through 2025 and 2026 it became power. The marker
events are concrete. Microsoft signed a 20-year power purchase
agreement to restart Three Mile Island Unit 1 to feed Azure AI
([Constellation press release, September 2024](https://www.constellationenergy.com/newsroom/2024/Constellation-to-Launch-Crane-Clean-Energy-Center-Restoring-Jobs-and-Carbon-Free-Power-to-The-Grid.html)),
the first US nuclear restart driven by AI demand. OpenAI, SoftBank,
and Oracle launched a $500B Stargate buildout in January 2025
([OpenAI Stargate announcement](https://openai.com/index/announcing-the-stargate-project/))
with $100B of immediate deployment. Amazon Web Services committed
to colocating with a Talen Energy nuclear plant in Pennsylvania
([AWS / Talen press release, March 2024](https://www.talenenergy.com/talen-energy-completes-sale-of-cumulus-data-data-center-campus-to-aws/)).
A chip order with NVIDIA might take 6 to 12 months. A new nuclear
reactor takes 5 to 10 years from interconnect filing to first
power; bringing a decommissioned one back online is faster but
still measured in years.

That asymmetry reshapes who can compete at frontier scale. Chip
procurement is a commercial problem with a clear counterparty.
Power procurement is a multi-year political and regulatory
problem involving utilities, grid operators, environmental
review, and in many cases state legislatures.

## The capex race and the new entrants

The hyperscaler set (Microsoft Azure, Google, AWS, Meta, Oracle)
continues to dominate frontier-scale capacity, but two new
categories have entered. The first is sovereign-state programs.
The UAE's G42 partnered with Microsoft for a $1.5B initial round
in 2024 and continues to expand under MGX
([G42 / Microsoft announcement, April 2024](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/microsoft-cloud/blog/2024/04/15/microsofts-1-5b-investment-in-uae-based-g42-expands-the-uaes-ai-capabilities/)).
Saudi Arabia's Humain, launched May 2025 under PIF, allocated
$10B for <G term="sovereign-compute">sovereign AI infrastructure</G>
([Humain launch coverage, Reuters, May 2025](https://www.reuters.com/world/middle-east/saudi-arabia-launches-ai-firm-humain-pif-2025-05-13/)).
The IndiaAI Mission committed $1.2B in March 2024 with explicit
language about subsidizing GPU access for Indian startups and
academia
([Indian government announcement, March 2024](https://www.meity.gov.in/static/uploads/2024/03/2c61b6dc4e2b8de5fde9fb1f7f4daf03.pdf)).

The second category is AI-native infrastructure operators that
sit between the hyperscalers and the long tail: CoreWeave (IPO'd
March 2025), Lambda, Crusoe, and Nebius. These run AI-purpose-
built campuses but lease capacity wholesale rather than serving
end users. Their bargaining position is improving because they
moved on power-and-siting before the hyperscalers fully woke up
to it.

## Counterweights, categorized properly

The decentralized response to the capex concentration comes in
two organizationally distinct shapes. They are often conflated;
they should not be.

**Organizational decentralization** is a market design where
many independent operators contribute capacity and a protocol
matches supply to demand. Akash Network bids GPU jobs against
node operators with a token-settled rail. io.net aggregates idle
GPUs from independent providers and exposes a single API. The
Bittensor compute subnets (Subnet 51 for compute, Subnet 27 for
training) reward operators in TAO for serving requests. The
operator set is permissionless, no single entity owns the capacity,
and the worst-case failure is degraded service rather than a
single-vendor outage.

**Physical decentralization** is when one company sites many
small facilities instead of one giant campus. Crusoe's original
play was modular data centers co-located with flare gas stacks in
North Dakota and Texas oil fields, capturing energy that was
otherwise being burned. That was physically distributed but
organizationally centralized: Crusoe is a single proprietary
company that owns and operates every site. The two models share
a "not the hyperscaler default" vibe but solve different problems.
Marketplace decentralization addresses operator-set diversity;
physical decentralization addresses siting and energy economics.

Crusoe itself illustrates that companies move on this spectrum.
As of 2025, Crusoe is the lead developer of the first Stargate
site in Abilene, Texas
([Stargate Abilene reporting, Bloomberg, March 2025](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-03-20/openai-s-stargate-site-in-texas-faces-power-challenges)),
a single concentrated multi-gigawatt campus. The company that
started as the poster child for distributed flare-gas siting is
now the contractor for the hyperscaler-default play. The category
label "decentralized" does not stick to a company across its
history; it sticks to the specific business model.

A third axis cross-cuts both. <G term="behind-the-meter">Behind-the-meter</G> siting (the data
center sits on the same side of the utility meter as the
generation, bypassing the <G term="grid-interconnect">grid interconnect queue</G>) is
geographically agnostic and can serve either model. Some
sovereign-state programs use behind-the-meter colocation to
sidestep their own regulators.

## What's open and what isn't

Almost no artifact at this layer is open-source in the <G term="osi">OSI</G>
sense. Data centers are buildings; transformers and switchgear
are commercial products; power purchase agreements are private
contracts. The relevant openness question at this layer is not
license terms but jurisdiction and operator concentration: whose
state has signing authority over the gigawatts, and how many
independent operators are in the set. That is why this layer
maps tightly to sovereignty (see the [sovereignty and
decentralization primitives layer](/stack/sovereignty-decentralization))
rather than to governance, which is about licensing further up
the stack.

## The editorial tension

Concentration versus distribution of gigawatts is the central
debate. Whether 5 GW of frontier compute lives in 4 hyperscaler
regions or 40 operators across 20 jurisdictions decides whether
sovereign-state or individual-scale open-source AI is technically
possible at frontier scale at all. The concentration side argues
that frontier training requires tightly-coupled, high-bandwidth
gigawatt-scale campuses that only hyperscalers and well-financed
states can build; the distribution side argues that <G term="inference">inference</G>,
<G term="fine-tuning">fine-tuning</G>, and even some training workloads can be served from
heterogeneous physically-distributed compute, and that the
sovereignty cost of running an open ecosystem on closed
infrastructure eventually dominates the efficiency cost of
running it on slower distributed alternatives. Both sides agree
the trajectory is uncertain; the disagreement is which side has
the lower long-run cost.
