## What this layer is

The substrate. Instruction set architectures (ISAs) and the physical
accelerators that execute matrix math. Two sub-questions live here, and they
are independent: is the ISA open, and is the accelerator architecture open.
RISC-V is an open ISA but most RISC-V chips on the market today are not AI
accelerators. NVIDIA H100s are closed accelerators on a closed ISA.
Tenstorrent is the rare datapoint trying to make both open, on the AI
accelerator side specifically.

Silicon is the slowest-moving layer in this stack. A new model trains in
months. A new runtime engine ships in weeks. A new chip takes three to five
years from tape-out to volume. The implication: every layer above silicon
inherits whatever lock-in the silicon vendor has accumulated, and that
lock-in compounds the longer the rest of the stack tolerates it.

## Open vs. closed

The closed side is NVIDIA. The economic story is not that NVIDIA makes the
only good chips; it is that the CUDA software ecosystem on top of those
chips has been compounding for fifteen years, and every other layer of the
AI stack has trained itself to assume CUDA. AMD's ROCm exists but lags by
years on developer experience and per-framework optimization. Intel's Gaudi
is competitive on some benchmarks but is tied to Intel as the sole vendor.
Apple's Neural Engine is proprietary and not aimed at training at scale.
Google's TPUs are not for sale. Amazon's Trainium and Inferentia are not
for sale outside AWS.

The open side is thin and recent. RISC-V is the open ISA, royalty-free and
governance-light, and is the substrate that countries and companies use
when they want to avoid licensing fees and export-control entanglement
with ARM or x86. SiFive is the leading commercial RISC-V IP vendor.
Tenstorrent is the company most aggressively trying to make open silicon
at the AI accelerator level: their Wormhole and upcoming Blackhole
products combine RISC-V CPUs with proprietary-then-opening Tensix tensor
cores.

There are partial datapoints in between. Cerebras builds wafer-scale
accelerators that are proprietary in design but disruptive in inference
economics. Groq builds LPUs that are proprietary but extraordinarily fast
on inference for specific model sizes. Neither is "open silicon" in the
Tenstorrent sense; they are alternatives to NVIDIA, not alternatives to
closed silicon.

## The lock-in vector

Closed silicon defeats every open layer above it. If you publish open
weights, you have done a service to research; you have not changed who
owns the means of running those weights in production. The economic model
for serving open weights still routes through a small number of companies
running hyperscaler infrastructure on NVIDIA chips, paying NVIDIA gross
margins of seventy-plus percent on the hardware.

The lock-in is two-layered. The hardware-level lock-in is real (you need
physical access to the chip) but is the smaller half. The software-level
lock-in (CUDA, cuDNN, cuBLAS, TensorRT, NCCL) is the larger half: even
when a competing chip exists, the porting cost of years of CUDA-optimized
kernels means the competing chip ships with two-year-old kernel quality,
which means it loses on benchmarks, which means nobody builds for it,
which means the kernels never get written. This is the canonical platform
lock-in pattern.
