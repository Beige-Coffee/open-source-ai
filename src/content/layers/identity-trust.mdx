## What this layer is

The cryptographic and hardware infrastructure that lets you verify what
a model is doing or who an agent is. It is a meta-layer because it
crosses every part of the production pipeline: trust anchors at silicon,
confidential computing at runtime, verifiable inference at the model
call, agent passports at the protocol layer.

This layer is also new enough that the standard nine-layer taxonomy
historically buried it under cross-cutting topics. Giving it a named
meta-layer makes the dependencies visible: when sovereignty arguments
require "you can prove the right model ran on your data," every piece
of evidence comes from a different sub-cluster here.

## What is in it

**Trusted Execution Environments (TEEs)**: Intel SGX, AMD SEV, ARM
TrustZone, Apple Secure Enclave, NVIDIA H100 Confidential Computing.
Hardware-attested isolated execution.

**Confidential computing services**: Phala Network (TEE-as-a-service),
Apple PCC (Private Cloud Compute), various AWS / Azure / GCP
confidential VM offerings.

**Verifiable inference (ZKML)**: EZKL (zero-knowledge proofs for ML
inference), Modulus Labs, Lagrange (ZK coprocessor for verifiable
compute, including DeepProve).

**Agent identity primitives**: agent passports (cryptographic
credentials for autonomous agents acting on a user's behalf),
decentralized identifiers (DIDs, W3C standard), Worldcoin / World ID
(proof-of-personhood), L402 macaroons as a capability-token primitive.

**an internal team-style productized trust anchors**: hardware-anchored personal-AI
appliances where the trust anchor and the runtime live together,
audited and privilege-separated (an internal team Aegis, the internal system).

## The lock-in vector

Identity and trust have a subtle lock-in: trust primitives depend on
silicon vendor cooperation. NVIDIA H100 Confidential Computing only
works on NVIDIA. Apple PCC only works in Apple's cloud. Intel SGX has
its own complexities. If verifiable inference becomes load-bearing for
sovereignty, the silicon-vendor monopoly at Layer 1 re-entrenches at
the trust layer.

The opposite framing: open-silicon counter-bets (Tenstorrent, RISC-V)
have to develop their own trust-anchor stories before the silicon
layer's open story is complete. As of 2026 this is mostly future
work. ZKML approaches (EZKL, Modulus, Lagrange) try to make trust
silicon-agnostic via pure cryptography, at substantial performance
cost.
