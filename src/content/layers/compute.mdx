## What this layer is

Where silicon physically lives and how it gets accessed. This layer has
three categories: self-hosted (you own the hardware), centralized cloud
(AWS, Azure, GCP, Oracle, CoreWeave, Lambda, plus the smaller dedicated AI
clouds), and decentralized compute marketplaces (Akash, io.net, Hyperbolic,
Bittensor compute subnets, Render, Gensyn). Each category trades different
things off against different things. Self-hosted is the most sovereign but
the least scalable. Centralized cloud is the most scalable but the least
sovereign. Decentralized is the most hopeful and the least proven.

This is the layer where decentralized infrastructure narratives from
adjacent fields (Bitcoin, Lightning, IPFS, Filecoin) most cleanly map to
existing crypto infrastructure. The question for the AI era is whether
permissionless compute is solvable, and at what point of the cost-quality
curve it becomes practical for real workloads.

## Open vs. closed

The closed side is the hyperscaler oligopoly. AWS, Azure, GCP, Oracle
(newly relevant via the Stargate joint venture), plus a small set of
dedicated AI-focused clouds like CoreWeave, Lambda Labs, Together AI on
the hosted side. These are not "closed" in the sense that they refuse to
run open software, but they are closed in the sense that they own the
customer relationship, the SLAs, and the ability to change pricing
unilaterally. Egress fees and lock-in pricing (committed-use discounts
that punish leaving) are the operational shape of the lock-in.

The decentralized open side is a small set of competing networks, each
with a different model. Akash Network is the longest-running, built on
Cosmos SDK with the AKT token and a reverse-auction marketplace. io.net
is Solana-based and aggregates GPU supply across DePIN networks. Hyperbolic
is more of a marketplace plus hosted inference. Bittensor's compute
subnets sit inside the TAO economy with emission-based incentives. Gensyn
is explicitly about decentralized training with cryptographic verification
of compute.

## The lock-in vector

Compute does not have a structural lock-in vector of its own; it inherits
silicon's lock-in (you can only decentralize what NVIDIA sells you) and
it is the layer most amenable to actual market competition. The interesting
sovereignty argument here is not "open vs. closed" so much as "concentrated
vs. distributed." Even if every provider runs the same NVIDIA chips,
having a hundred providers means no single one owns the relationship.

The honest critique of decentralized compute today: it is cheaper for
spot workloads but worse for reliability, and the gap between "experiment"
and "production" is wide. The summit-grade framing on this layer: decentralized
compute is closer to ready than most people think for inference, still far
from ready for training at scale, and the gap is closing fastest at the
inference end.
