## What this layer is

Definitions, licenses, foundations, standards bodies, and the procedural
infrastructure that decides what "open" means in the AI context.
Governance is meta-relative-to-the-stack: it does not produce silicon,
weights, or runtime, but it produces the definitions by which the other
layers get judged.

The dominant body is the Open Source Initiative (OSI), which has
historically defined "open source" for software and which has now
extended that work into AI. OSAID v1.0 (Open Source AI Definition,
October 2024) is the first formal definition of "open source AI." It
is contested in specific ways, especially around the requirement (or
absence of requirement) for full data disclosure. The next OSAID
revision cycle is scheduled to overlap Q4 2026.

Other bodies that matter: the Linux Foundation as a neutral hosting and
governance ground, AAIF (Agentic AI Foundation) as the agent-and-protocol
focused subsidiary, the Apache Foundation for Apache 2.0 stewardship,
and the lab-specific license families (Llama Community License, Gemma
Terms, Mistral Research Tiers).

## Open vs. closed

The open governance landscape is small and structured. **OSI** is the
long-standing body for software definitions, now extending into AI via
OSAID. **Linux Foundation** is the neutral hosting body that owns AAIF,
A2A, and various other AI projects. The **Free Software Foundation** has
a different (stricter) posture but is less active in current AI
governance discussions. The **Apache Software Foundation** stewards
Apache 2.0, the most-used license for open AI projects.

The contested governance question for AI specifically is OSAID v1.0's
requirement: "weights, code, and data information sufficient for a
skilled person to recreate a substantially equivalent system." Critics
(especially Bradley Kuhn at the Software Freedom Conservancy and parts
of the academic open-data community) argue that "data information
sufficient" allows for closed-data models to claim open-source status.
Supporters (OSI's Stefano Maffulli, AAIF) argue that requiring full data
would make the definition impractical given the legal landscape around
data licensing.

The closed side at the governance layer is the absence of governance:
lab-specific licenses (Llama Community License, Gemma Terms, Mistral
Research Tiers) operate outside the OSI process and accumulate their own
ecosystem-by-default. The dynamic: the more models ship under non-OSI
licenses, the more "open" stops meaning "OSI-approved" and starts
meaning "whatever the dominant lab calls open."

## The lock-in vector

Governance lock-in is the longest-cycle but most-foundational lock-in.
Once a definition is in the field and adopted, changing it is a
multi-year project. The OSAID v1.0 is the definition through approximately
2026; if Llama Community License-style models become "open AI" in popular
usage, OSAID risks being a definition nobody enforces, which is the
worst possible outcome for definitional rigor.

The constructive position: definitions matter, and the AAIF and OSI
should be tightening the definitions rather than loosening them. The
2026 OSAID revision cycle is the operational moment.
