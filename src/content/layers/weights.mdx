## What this layer is

The model files. The trained weights are the artifact most often labeled
"open" in popular conversation, and they are also the most superficial
layer of openness. Having the weights means you can run the model,
fine-tune it, and inspect it. It does not mean you can re-train it,
reproduce it, or audit how it was trained. Most "open-weights" models
are open at this layer and closed at Data (Layer 3) and at training-code
(implicitly part of Data or Training).

The weights layer is also where "open" hides the most license diversity.
Apache 2.0 is genuinely open. The Llama Community License is
source-available, not open-source; it carries acceptable-use restrictions
and a 700M MAU clause that Meta can revoke. Gemma's terms are similarly
source-available. DeepSeek and Qwen and Mistral mostly use Apache 2.0 or
MIT for their smaller releases and progressively more restrictive
licenses for their flagship releases. The OSAID v1.0 conversation matters
here because the definition decides which of these counts as "open
source AI."

## Open vs. closed

The open-weights ecosystem in 2026 has converged on a small set of model
families, mostly from one of four sources. The US labs (Meta's Llama,
Microsoft's Phi, Google's Gemma). The Chinese labs (Qwen from Alibaba,
DeepSeek, Kimi from Moonshot, GLM from Zhipu, Yi from 01.AI). The
European labs (Mistral, plus the truly-open AI2 with OLMo). And the
Middle Eastern labs (Falcon from TII in the UAE).

Llama is the most-used family but also the most-licensed. Mistral
started Apache 2.0 (Mistral 7B, Mixtral 8x7B) and has progressively
reserved more restrictive terms for flagship releases. Qwen has been
surprisingly aggressive at the open end: Qwen 2.5 and Qwen 3 shipped
under Apache 2.0 in many sizes. DeepSeek's V2, V3, and R1 releases reset
the cost-quality curve and ship under permissive licenses. OLMo is the
only family in the open-weights set that meets the strictest reading of
"open AI" because the data, code, and weights are all published.

The closed side is what the public discourse calls "frontier AI":
GPT-class at OpenAI, Claude at Anthropic, Gemini at Google. Plus the
API-only flagships of every lab that also ships open-weights (Mistral
Large, Cohere's flagship, the closed tiers of every Chinese lab). The
dynamic is that the same lab often ships an open tier (small, recent
generation) and a closed tier (largest, current generation).

## The lock-in vector

Weights have weaker lock-in than silicon or runtime, but they have one
specific lock-in: license restrictions. The Llama Community License is
the canonical example. Meta retains the right to revoke the license for
entities above the 700M MAU threshold, retains an acceptable-use list
that has been used to selectively restrict downstream use, and reserves
the right to update terms. This is structurally different from Apache
2.0, where the rights you receive at download time are durable.

The summit-grade framing here: "open weights" is necessary but not
sufficient, and the question is which license you actually got. The
strict-reading position is that only OSAID-compliant releases should be
called "open AI," and the rest should be called "source-available AI."
