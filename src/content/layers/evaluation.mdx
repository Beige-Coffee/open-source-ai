## What this layer is

How we measure progress. The benchmarks, harnesses, leaderboards, and
evaluation pipelines that decide which models are "better." Evaluation
is a meta-layer rather than a stack layer because it observes the rest
of the stack rather than sitting between two production-pipeline layers.
You can describe AI progress without naming evaluation; you cannot
report progress without it.

The interesting fact about evaluation in 2026 is that the open ecosystem
is in mostly-good shape here. lm-evaluation-harness (EleutherAI) is the
de facto reference implementation of dozens of standard evals; the
HuggingFace Open LLM Leaderboard ran v1 then v2; LMSYS Chatbot Arena
(now LMArena) is the most-respected human-preference evaluation. The
closed labs run their own private evals on private holdouts, but the
public evals are largely open.

What is not in good shape is the contamination problem (models trained
on the eval data score artificially well) and the saturation problem
(most public benchmarks have been saturated past the point of useful
signal). The frontier of evaluation has moved toward dynamic / arena-style
evals (LMArena), contamination-aware evals (LiveBench), and held-out
reasoning evals (FrontierMath, GPQA Diamond, HLE).

## Open vs. closed

The open side has three categories. **Benchmarks** (the eval datasets
and scoring functions): MMLU, MMLU-Pro, HumanEval, HumanEval+,
LiveCodeBench, SWE-bench, GPQA, FrontierMath, HLE, GSM8K, HellaSwag.
**Harnesses** (the code that runs evals): lm-evaluation-harness (the
standard), HELM (Stanford CRFM), Inspect (UK AISI). **Leaderboards**
(the public ranking): LMArena (gold standard), Open LLM Leaderboard
(archived), LiveBench (contamination-aware), Artificial Analysis
(commercial aggregator).

The closed side is **private holdout sets** at frontier labs. The labs
claim their models are good in part by reference to these internal
scores. The public has no way to verify or compare. The "we got 92% on
internal benchmark X" claims in lab announcements often refer to
private holdouts.

## The lock-in vector

Evaluation is not a clean lock-in vector in the silicon or runtime
sense; nobody is locked in to a benchmark. But there is an analogous
structural problem: if frontier labs use private holdouts to claim
leadership, the only way to verify is to trust the labs. That is a
soft-lock-in on the narrative. The open ecosystem can chase whatever
public benchmarks exist, but if those public benchmarks are saturated
or contaminated, the public benchmarks stop being able to distinguish
the frontier from the open-weights frontier.

The framing here is modest. Evaluation is the meta-layer where the open
ecosystem is doing best, and the residual problem is contamination and
saturation, not closure. A constructive position: the AAIF or a similar
neutral body should coordinate shared, refreshing eval datasets as a
public good.
