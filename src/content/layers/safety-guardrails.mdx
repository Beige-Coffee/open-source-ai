## What this layer is

The tools and techniques for constraining what a model or agent is
allowed to do. Safety is a meta-layer because constraints apply across
the production pipeline: at training time (RLHF, constitutional methods),
at inference time (output classifiers like Llama Guard), and at agent
runtime (sandbox-escape evaluations, capability-token enforcement).

In 2026 the open ecosystem at this layer is growing but is not yet at
parity with the closed labs' internal safety stacks (which are
proprietary by design and increasingly capability-evaluation-heavy).

## What is in it

**Output classifiers**: Llama Guard (Meta, open-weights), NeMo Guardrails
(NVIDIA, Apache 2.0), various smaller open classifiers for specific
prompt-injection patterns.

**Training-time techniques**: Constitutional AI (Anthropic, methodology
public), RLHF tooling in TRL, DPO and KTO and related preference-tuning
methods.

**Agent-level evaluation**: METR (Model Evaluation and Threat Research)
sandbox-escape benchmarks, AISI Inspect framework for safety evaluations,
Apollo Research's scheming-monitor work.

**Capability-token enforcement**: the internal system-style privilege-separated
kernels for personal-AI servers; the closest mainstream parallel is
TEE-based sandboxing.

## The lock-in vector

Safety at the open-ecosystem layer is mostly tooling and methodology;
there is little structural lock-in. The risk is reverse: the closed
labs invest enormously in internal safety evaluations that they do not
publish, which lets them claim a safety story the open ecosystem cannot
independently verify.

The constructive position: safety evaluation suites should themselves
be open (the way lm-evaluation-harness is open for capability), so that
the open ecosystem can produce auditable safety claims. AISI Inspect
is the closest existing model.
