This is one of three load-bearing claims about lock-in vectors that
this site is built around. The other two: closed runtime defeats open
silicon, and closed agents defeat open weights. They chain together
into a single argument that the open AI stack is only as sovereign as
its weakest open layer.

## The claim

You can publish open weights for the highest-capability model in the
world. That is a service to research and a real artifact. It does not
change who owns the production economics of running the model.

The reason is that "the model" in production is a forward pass through
billions of parameters running on physical silicon. The silicon is
overwhelmingly NVIDIA H100s and H200s. The software stack on top of
that silicon is CUDA, cuDNN, cuBLAS, NCCL, TensorRT-LLM. NVIDIA's gross
margin on the hardware is north of 70%. The hyperscalers (AWS, Azure,
GCP, Oracle) buy the chips and resell the inference. The open-weights
model passes through both layers paying both tolls.

So the open-weights release is best thought of as the model becoming
free. The cost shifts from "model API" to "compute and runtime."
Whoever controls the compute and runtime captures most of the surplus.

## Why the lock-in is two-layered

The hardware-level lock-in is real but it is the smaller half. Even if
a credible non-NVIDIA accelerator existed at parity, the porting cost
of years of CUDA-optimized kernels means the competing chip ships with
two-year-old kernel quality. Two-year-old kernels lose on benchmarks.
Losing on benchmarks means nobody builds for it. Nobody building for
it means the kernels never get written.

This is the canonical platform lock-in pattern. It has been in motion
at NVIDIA for fifteen years.

The software-level lock-in is the larger half. CUDA is a moat made of
compounding-developer-attention. Every framework (PyTorch, JAX, TF),
every inference engine (vLLM, SGLang, TensorRT-LLM, llama.cpp), every
research codebase assumes CUDA as the default backend. AMD's ROCm
exists. Intel's oneAPI exists. They lag by years on developer
experience and per-framework optimization.

(Reading: CUDA, A Developer's Best Practices) for the canonical primer
on what the moat actually is at the hardware-software boundary.

## Where the open counter-bet lives

There are two open counter-bets at the silicon layer. Both are real,
both are early.

(Reading: RISC-V: An Open Architecture Atlas) is the open ISA: royalty-free,
governance-light, the substrate that countries and companies use when
they want to avoid licensing fees and export-control entanglement with
ARM or x86. Most RISC-V chips on the market today are not AI
accelerators; they are CPUs and embedded.

(Project: tenstorrent) is the open accelerator. Wormhole and Blackhole
combine RISC-V CPUs with proprietary-then-opening Tensix tensor cores.
tt-metal and tt-forge are open. Jim Keller leads. This is the rare
datapoint trying to make both the ISA and the accelerator open at the
AI-accelerator level specifically.

Neither has reached production-scale parity with NVIDIA in 2026. The
sovereignty argument requires they get there.

## The local-AI corollary

This claim has a sharper edge for local AI specifically. For cloud AI,
silicon is invisible to the user; you hit OpenAI's API and you do not
know or care whether the inference ran on H100s or B200s or TPUs.

For local AI, silicon is the entire point. Every other layer (open
weights, open runtime, open agent) presupposes that a user can buy or
own hardware capable of running the thing. In 2026 the de facto answer
to "I want to run a 70B model locally on hardware I own" is "buy a Mac
with 64GB+ unified memory." That is an Apple-locked answer. Apple's
unified-memory architecture is the only consumer-grade hardware with
enough memory bandwidth and capacity in a form factor a normal person
can put on a desk. Strix Halo is the first credible non-Apple
alternative; Tenstorrent is the longer bet.

If consumer-class accelerators with enough memory are only made by
Apple (closed ISA, closed accelerator, closed software stack), then
"local AI" in 2026 is structurally an Apple ecosystem story plus a
small-model llama.cpp-on-anything story, and not much else.

## What follows

If this claim is right, the silicon layer (Layer 1) is the strongest
lock-in vector in the stack and the hardest to displace, because
silicon cycles are 3-5 years from tape-out to volume. Every layer
above it inherits its constraints. The open-silicon counter-bets are
load-bearing for sovereignty in a way they are not for raw capability.

(Layer: silicon) is where this lives operationally.

The companion claims:

- Closed runtime defeats open silicon, even if the silicon goes open
- Closed agents defeat open weights, even if the runtime goes open
