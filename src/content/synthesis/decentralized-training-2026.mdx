For most of the LLM era, training a frontier-class model required a
hyperscaler-class cluster: tens of thousands of GPUs in one
datacenter with high-bandwidth NVLink interconnects between them. The
sovereignty argument has always assumed this would change. In 2026 it
has, partially. This essay names where it has and where it has not.

## The technical reason it was hard

Frontier training is dominated by two operations: forward passes
(through the network to compute loss) and backward passes (computing
gradients, then synchronizing them across workers so the next forward
pass starts from the same weights). The gradient synchronization step
moves tens to hundreds of gigabytes per training step across the
cluster. A typical step takes seconds to minutes; the network has to
keep up.

Inside a datacenter, NVLink and NVSwitch give you 900 GB/s+ between
GPUs. Between racks, InfiniBand or 400G Ethernet gives you 400-800
Gbps. Between datacenters or across the public internet, you get
bandwidth that varies from "fine for a video call" to "sometimes
nothing" with high latency.

Naive distributed training over the public internet falls over not
because the math is wrong but because the gradient sync step takes
longer than the forward pass it is supposed to enable.

## What changed (the techniques that actually work)

Three categories of techniques have made distributed training over
the internet feasible at non-frontier scale.

**Bandwidth-efficient gradient compression.** Instead of sending
full-precision gradients, send compressed approximations. Lossy
algorithms reduce bandwidth requirements 100-1000x with acceptable
training quality loss. (Reading: DisTrO: Distributed Training Over-the-Internet)
from Nous Research is the best-documented current technique.

**Asynchronous local training.** Each node trains for many local
steps before synchronizing with peers. Reduces synchronization
frequency by orders of magnitude. Trade-off: workers diverge between
syncs and the convergence dynamics are not as well understood as
synchronous training.

**Hybrid topologies.** A small set of well-connected anchor nodes
plus many lower-bandwidth peripheral nodes. Anchors do the heavy
synchronization; peripherals do local work and contribute back
periodically.

## The current public results

(Project: prime-intellect)'s INTELLECT-1 (released November 2024) was
the first decentralized training run of a 10B+ model across
continents, on commodity hardware over the public internet. Reproducible
methodology published. (Reading: INTELLECT-1 Technical Report)

Prime Intellect's INTELLECT-2 followed (mid-2025) at 32B parameters
with reinforcement-learning post-training distributed globally.
(Reading: INTELLECT-2: 32B Globally-Distributed RL)

(Project: templar) (Bittensor subnet) ran a 72B distributed pretrain
from scratch on Bittensor, the most ambitious distributed training
run to date.

(Project: pluralis), (Project: gensyn) are at earlier stages with
adjacent-but-distinct technical approaches. The Bittensor compute
subnets and the Akash GPU marketplace provide the underlying compute
substrate for many of these runs.

## The honest ceiling

What works in 2026:

- Pretraining 10-70B models on globally distributed commodity
  hardware. Achievable by any sufficiently-funded coordinator.
- Post-training (RLHF, DPO, RL) on distributed infrastructure.
  Less bandwidth-intensive than pretraining; works at the same
  scale.

What does not work in 2026:

- Frontier-scale pretraining (1T+ parameters in 2026 numbers).
  The bandwidth and synchronization requirements still exceed what
  the public internet provides at acceptable cost.
- Catching up to centralized frontier training on iso-cost,
  iso-time. Centralized clusters use the bandwidth-efficient
  techniques too, so the gap stays roughly an order of magnitude on
  current evidence.

The interesting question is not whether decentralized training will
displace centralized frontier training (it will not in 2026, probably
not in 2027). The interesting question is **what specific category of
model becomes feasible to train without hyperscaler approval at the
current capability tier.**

## What this unlocks

Three things become possible at the current decentralized-training
capability tier:

**Domain-specialized models.** A 30-70B model trained on a specific
corpus (legal, medical, scientific, regional language) without
hyperscaler dependency. Sovereignty matters here because the corpus
itself may not be transferable to a hyperscaler (privacy, licensing,
strategic value).

**Frontier-shadowing community models.** A community of researchers
or a nation-state that wants a model in the same generation as the
frontier without paying hyperscaler economics. INTELLECT-1 is the
existence proof. The model is not as capable as the contemporary
frontier but it is independent of any hyperscaler relationship.

**Recovery infrastructure.** If a frontier lab takes a model offline
or restricts access (geography, terms-of-use), a community can train
a near-equivalent without that lab's permission. The capability is
not the point; the existence of the option is.
