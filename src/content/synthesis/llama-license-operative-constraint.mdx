Most public discussion of "open weights" uses Llama as the canonical
example. Most legal analysis of "open weights" uses Llama as the
canonical counter-example. Both are correct depending on the
definition. The point of this essay: which definition you use
determines whether Llama is actually open enough to anchor a
sovereign-AI thesis.

## The five operative clauses

The Llama Community License is structured as a conditional grant.
Meta gives you broad rights, then attaches conditions that retain
operative control. Five clauses do most of the work:

**1. The 700M MAU clause.** "If, on the Llama 3 version release date,
the monthly active users of the products or services made available
by or for Licensee, or Licensee's affiliates, is greater than 700
million monthly active users in the preceding calendar month, you
must request a license from Meta." Translation: any company at
TikTok / Snapchat / WhatsApp scale needs Meta's permission. Most
companies are not at that scale. Five companies are. The clause is
designed to require Meta's permission specifically from those
competitors.

**2. The acceptable-use policy.** A list of prohibited uses
maintained by Meta and revisable by Meta. Examples include certain
biological, chemical, and weapons research; certain
generative-content categories. The list itself is reasonable; the
governance shape is unilateral. Meta updates it, you comply.

**3. The no-competing-models clause.** "You will not use the Llama
Materials or any output or results of the Llama Materials to improve
any other large language model (excluding Llama 3 or derivative works
thereof)." Translation: you cannot use Llama outputs to train a
different model family. Most fine-tuning is fine. Distillation into
a non-Llama base model is not.

**4. Geographic exclusions.** Some Llama versions have been
restricted in the EU pending AI Act compliance work; some have been
restricted in other jurisdictions for export-control reasons. The
license itself does not directly impose this; the surrounding
release decisions do. End user impact is the same: the model may not
be available where you are.

**5. The right to update.** Meta retains the right to modify the
license terms with notice. Apache 2.0 does not have this. Once you
accept Apache 2.0, the rights you received are durable. The Llama
license is conditionally durable.

## The structural framing

Apache 2.0 (and MIT, BSD, MPL) are **durable rights**: once you
download the artifact under the license, the rights you received do
not change. The license cannot be revoked retroactively. This is the
property that makes Apache 2.0 software useful as critical
infrastructure.

The Llama license is **conditional permission**: you are permitted to
use the artifact subject to a list of conditions Meta controls. The
conditions can be revised. The permission can be terminated for
violation.

This is a real difference. It is not pedantic. The question for any
sovereign-AI thesis is: which kind of grant is your stack built on?

A useful analogy: Apache 2.0 is like running your own Bitcoin node;
the rules you operate under are durable and you can audit them. The
Llama license is like having a friendly bank account; the rules you
operate under are conditional and the bank can change them.

## What the OSI says

(Reading: The Llama Community License, Annotated) is the Open Source
Initiative's published position: Llama is not open source by the OSI
definition. Specifically, the 700M MAU clause is field-of-use
discrimination (OSD #6); the acceptable-use policy is a usage
restriction (OSD #6); the no-competing-models clause is
field-of-endeavor discrimination (OSD #6 again).

By the OSI's published Open Source AI Definition v1.0
(October 2024), Llama is also not open source AI: the data is not
disclosed, and the use restrictions disqualify it independently.

Meta's position is that the license is a reasonable balance between
openness and managed risk. Meta is not wrong that the balance is
reasonable for Meta. Meta is also not openly licensed.

## Where this matters operationally

For a Bitcoin-OSS funder considering AI grants, the operative
question is whether to fund projects built on Llama or projects built
on more permissively-licensed weights. Three considerations:

**Mistral's drift.** Mistral started with Apache 2.0 (Mistral 7B,
Mixtral 8x7B). Their flagship releases have moved toward
research-only or commercial-tier licenses. The Apache 2.0 small
models are still durable; the flagship weights are not. (Project:
mistral-7b)

**Qwen's posture.** (Project: qwen3) ships under Apache 2.0 in many
sizes. Surprisingly aggressive at the open end. Currently the most-
downloaded open-weights family.

**OLMo as the strict-reading exemplar.** (Project: olmo) is the only
major model family in 2026 that meets the strictest reading of
"open AI" because the data, code, and weights are all published.
(Reading: OLMo 2: A Truly Open Language Model)

For builders: pick the license posture that matches the durability
your project needs. For grants: weigh whether the recipient's
license posture matches the funder's thesis. For policy: the OSAID
v2.0 cycle (overlapping the Sovereign AI Summit window in late 2026)
will decide which of these counts as "open source AI" in the
canonical definition. (Reading: The Open Source AI Definition v1.0)

## What follows

If "open weights" is allowed to mean "Llama-licensed," the OSI's
definition becomes a definition nobody enforces, which is the worst
possible outcome for definitional rigor. The constructive position:
distinguish source-available weights from open-source weights every
time you write about them. Use the durability question as the test.
